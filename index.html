An excerpt from I Only Cook Supper

Chapter One — The Key

She handed me a key on a length of kitchen string and told me she only cooked supper. She said it once, the way she said most important things, and then she turned back to the stove as if the matter were closed. I was nine. The key was warm from her pocket, and it stayed warm the whole walk to school, knocking against my breastbone like a second, smaller heart.

What she meant, I would spend the next thirty years working out. Breakfast you fend for yourself. Lunch is the world's problem. But supper — supper is the one meal a family owes each other, the one you come home for, the one nobody eats alone if there's any way around it. In our house the day did not truly end until the table did.

This is a book about that table. About the mill town that fed us and nearly finished us, about the recipes I've carried like heirlooms, and about a mother who could say more with a set of instructions for pot roast than most people manage in a lifetime of talking.
