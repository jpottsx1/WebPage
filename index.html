From I Only Cook Supper

My mother delivered the news without ceremony. That was her way.

She was standing at the kitchen counter with a Du Maurier, Large, King Size burning in the glass ashtray by the sink, looking out the window at something in the yard that was either very interesting or not interesting at all. With Carol Potts it was sometimes hard to tell which. I’d come in from playing outside on a Saturday, starving, six years old, give or take a few months. I hadn’t even been alive two thousand days.

“I only cook supper.”

I heard it. I processed it. I did not immediately understand it.

She picked up the cigarette, took a long pull, set it back in the ashtray. Then she turned and looked at me with an expression I’d come to recognize over the next several decades as her expression for and that’s that. Not unkind. Not apologetic. Just efficient. Carol Potts was, above all else, efficient.

I went back outside that afternoon turning the sentence over, and got it wrong. I thought she was talking about lunch. She was talking about the rest of my childhood.

Not once in those two thousand days had anyone suggested the free ride might end. I’d been fed, more or less continually, since birth (breakfast appeared, lunch appeared, supper appeared at six with the reliability of the CBC news) and I had contributed nothing whatsoever to the production of any of it. I considered the arrangement correct and permanent. Two meals a day, gone, just like that. The ground was shifting under me and I didn’t even feel it move.

Existence as a latchkey kid came with exactly three rules:

  1. A key around your neck.
  2. No use of the stovetop.
  3. Always be home for supper.

That was it. That was the whole contract. Everything else was mine to figure out.

About the Author

Jeffrey Potts

Jeffrey Potts is a bilingual writer fluent in both French and English, who grew up in the vibrant mill town of Cornwall, Ontario. He graduated from University with a Bachelor of Arts in Economics, a foundation that has informed his diverse and accomplished career.

Jeffrey’s professional journey began in the late 1980s, managing campus community radio stations, a role that honed his communication and organizational skills. He later transitioned to teaching, working as a high school educator before co-owning a successful computer consulting firm in downtown Toronto. This entrepreneurial venture eventually led him to the United States during the dot-com boom of the 1990s, where he thrived as a management consultant.

After gaining extensive experience in the fast-paced world of American business, Jeffrey returned to Canada to join one of the country’s top five management consultancies. However, weary of the relentless business travel that came with the job, he and his wife decided to embrace a quieter life, settling in the picturesque community of Prince Edward County.

Now drawing inspiration from his rich professional background and the serene surroundings of his new home, Jeffrey focuses on his passion for writing, sharing stories that blend his deep understanding of human connections and the complexities of modern life.
